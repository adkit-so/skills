# Crafting Ads

## How to Use This Guide

You are an AI agent helping a user create effective Meta ad creatives. This guide is your playbook. Follow these principles:

- **Lead with questions, not lectures.** Understand the user's situation before giving advice.
- **Be specific and actionable.** Generic tips are useless. Tailor every recommendation to what the user is actually building.
- **Catch mistakes early.** If the user is heading toward a policy violation or a format that won't perform, flag it immediately — before they waste time producing it.

Start every ad creation session by understanding what the user already has:

> **Ask the user:** Do you have existing ads or creative assets to work with? Or are you starting from scratch?
>
> **Ask the user:** What format are you planning? Image, video, or not sure?
>
> **If not sure:** Recommend starting with a static image + the hybrid trick (add subtle animation to get the video algorithm boost). It's the fastest path to a testable creative.

## Ad Policy Compliance

Address this early. Users often don't realize their creative will get rejected until it's too late — and repeated violations have real consequences.

> **Remind the user:** Meta scans both the ad creative AND the landing page it links to. A compliant ad pointing to a non-compliant page still gets rejected.

### What Gets Ads Rejected

- **Misleading claims** — exaggerated results, fake urgency ("Only 3 left!"), unsubstantiated statistics
- **Before/after imagery** — especially in health, fitness, beauty. Even subtle implications of transformation can trigger rejection
- **Prohibited content** — weapons, drugs, discriminatory targeting, adult content, surveillance tools. Full list in Meta's Advertising Standards, but the above are the most common traps for legitimate advertisers
- **Personal attributes** — ad copy that asserts or implies knowledge of personal characteristics ("Are you struggling with anxiety?" gets rejected; "Many people struggle with anxiety" is fine)

### Escalation Risk

Rejections aren't just annoying — they compound:

1. **First rejection**: ad disapproved, can edit and resubmit
2. **Repeated rejections**: account flagged, review times increase, more ads get auto-rejected
3. **Pattern of violations**: ad account restricted or permanently banned
4. **Cross-account**: Meta tracks by person/entity, not just account. Creating a new account doesn't reset your record

### Silent Performance Penalties

Even without explicit rejection, low-quality creatives raise costs:

- **Text-heavy images** — Meta penalizes images where text covers more than ~20% of the area. Not rejected, but CPMs go up significantly
- **Clickbait patterns** — withholding information to bait clicks ("You won't believe what happened next") gets detected and penalized
- **Engagement bait** — asking for likes/shares/comments in the ad reduces delivery
- **Low relevance scores** — if users consistently hide or report your ad, Meta throttles delivery

> **When reviewing the user's creative:** Check for these issues before they publish. It's much easier to fix copy and visuals now than to recover a flagged account later.

## Creative Formats

### Format Comparison

| Format | Aspect Ratio | Resolution | Best For |
|--------|-------------|------------|----------|
| Square image | 1:1 | 1080x1080 | Feed posts, simple static ads |
| Landscape image | 16:9 | 1920x1080 | Stories (limited use) |
| Feed video | 4:5 | 1080x1350 | Feed — occupies maximum screen space |
| Reels/Stories video | 9:16 | 1080x1920 | Reels, Stories, full-screen placements |

> **When the user picks a format:** Confirm they know where it will appear. A 1:1 image works in-feed but wastes space in Stories. A 9:16 video dominates Reels but gets cropped in-feed. Match the format to the placement.

### Video vs. Image

Meta heavily favors video. Videos keep users on-platform longer, so the algorithm rewards them with cheaper delivery and more reach. A 10-second video gets far more engagement time than any static image.

**That said: a good static image beats a bad video.** The message matters more than the format.

### The Hybrid Trick

Static content + animated background = Meta counts it as video. Take your image creative, add subtle motion (animated background, text animation, gentle zoom), and you get the video algorithm boost without full video production.

> **Recommend this when:** The user wants video performance but doesn't have video production capacity. It's the highest ROI format for most small teams.

### Safe Zones (9:16 Video)

Design for 9:16 but expect cropping. Meta auto-crops to 4:5 in the feed.

- **Top 15%**: Cropped in feed view — no critical content
- **Bottom 20%**: Overlaid by Reels UI (username, CTA buttons, description)
- **Center 65%**: Safe zone — all key messaging goes here

If your video works in both 9:16 and 4:5 without losing meaning, it works everywhere.

> **If the user is designing a 9:16 creative:** Walk them through the safe zones. Ask them to check: "Would your message still land if I cropped the top and bottom off?"

## Hooks & Scroll Stoppers

### The 1-Second Rule

Users decide in one second whether to keep scrolling or engage. Every ad lives or dies in that first moment. This is the single most important skill in paid advertising.

The visual hierarchy on Meta:
1. **Image/video** — first thing seen (videos autoplay)
2. **Headline** — scanned next
3. **Primary text** — read only if hooked

### What Makes a Hook Work

A hook must stand out relative to the surrounding content. If every ad in the feed uses bright red, bright red no longer stands out. Context matters.

### Hook Types

**Relatable** — The viewer instantly thinks "this is about me."
> Example: An ad showing the Facebook "account restricted" icon. Anyone who's been banned recognizes it immediately. The copy reads: "Tired of having your ad account blocked?" Instant recognition, instant relevance.

**Unexpected/Shocking** — Something visually unusual that breaks the scroll pattern.
> Example: QuillBot ad showing meat going through a grinder, captioned "my brain when I try to write sentences on my own." Zero connection to the product visually, but impossible to scroll past. The hook buys 1-2 seconds, then the ad does its job.

**Emotional** — Triggers a feeling before the viewer processes the message.
> Example: A children's math app ad with "This really helped my son" as the caption. Parents with struggling kids feel an immediate emotional pull.

**Sensory** — Leverages visual satisfaction, ASMR, or tactile appeal.
> Example: BlendJet's ad with satisfying pouring sounds, whispered voiceover, and smooth visual textures. Comments filled with "this ad is amazing" — people voluntarily engage.

> **Help the user pick a hook type:** Ask what kind of emotional response their audience would react to. A B2B SaaS audience responds to relatable frustration. A consumer product might benefit from sensory or unexpected hooks. There's no universal best — it depends on who's scrolling.

### Key Principle

Your ad competes with entertainment, not other ads. Users are on social media for dopamine, not shopping. If your ad looks like an ad, it gets skipped. If it looks like content, it gets watched.

## Writing Ad Copy

### Sell the Click, Not the Product

Ad copy has one job: make the user click. You are not closing a sale — you are earning a landing page visit. Every sentence should move them toward that click.

> **Remind the user:** The ad doesn't need to explain everything. It needs to make someone curious enough to click. Save the full pitch for the landing page.

### The Copy Framework

Walk the user through this step by step. Don't dump the whole framework at once — work through each part with them.

> **Step 1 — Ask:** Who is your audience? Be specific. "Small business owners" is too broad. "Solo consultants charging $150+/hr who rely on referrals" is usable.
>
> **Step 2 — Ask:** What's their pain point? What are they frustrated about right now? What have they tried that didn't work?
>
> **Step 3 — Ask:** How does your product solve that? In one sentence, what changes for them?
>
> **Step 4 — Ask:** What would make someone hesitate to click? Price? Trust? Complexity? Then write a line that neutralizes that objection.
>
> **Step 5 — Write the CTA together.** Tell them exactly what to do next. Include the link.

The resulting structure:

1. **Identify the audience** — Call out who this is for so the right people self-select
2. **State the problem** — Describe the pain they're experiencing right now
3. **Offer the solution** — Present your product/service as the answer
4. **Remove objections** — Address the top 1-2 reasons they'd hesitate
5. **Call to action** — Tell them exactly what to do next (include the link)

### Example Breakdown

> *"People selling done-for-you services, 1-on-1 coaching, consulting..."* — Audience identification
>
> *"Struggling to get consistent clients without relying on referrals?"* — Problem
>
> *"We built a system that..."* — Solution
>
> *"No long-term contracts. Cancel anytime."* — Objection removal
>
> *"Click below to see how it works"* — CTA

This ad used a bright green background as its creative — nothing fancy, just a scroll-stopping color with the copy doing all the heavy lifting.

### Copy Tips

- **Each sentence must lead to the next.** If a sentence doesn't pull the reader forward, cut it.
- **Emojis help readability.** They break up text walls and add visual anchors. Not mandatory, but effective.
- **Remove everything unnecessary.** If a word doesn't serve the hook, the argument, or the CTA — delete it.
- **Longer copy works** if the reader is hooked. Length is not the problem; boring is the problem.

> **When reviewing the user's copy:** Read it out loud (or simulate that). Does every sentence make you want to read the next one? If any line feels like filler, flag it.

## Getting Inspiration

### Meta Ads Library

Go to `facebook.com/ads/library`. Search any brand to see every ad they're currently running.

- **Strength**: Volume — see dozens of variants from any advertiser
- **Limitation**: You can only search brands you already know, and there are no performance metrics
- **Proxy for success**: The longer an ad has been running, the more likely it's profitable

> **Suggest this when:** The user has competitors they want to study. Help them identify which ads have been running longest (those are the winners).

### Build a Swipe File

The best ad inspiration comes from being a regular user. Scroll your feeds (Facebook, Instagram, TikTok) and when something stops you:

1. Save the link immediately
2. Analyze *why* it caught your attention — was it unexpected? Relatable? Visually satisfying?
3. Note the hook type, copy structure, and creative format
4. Add it to your swipe file

Do this regularly. Over weeks and months, you build a library of proven patterns you can adapt. Dedicate 1-2 hours every few weeks specifically to this.

### The Meta-Skill

Advertising intuition is built, not taught. The principles above give you a framework, but real skill comes from studying hundreds of ads, testing your own, and developing a feel for what works in your specific market.
